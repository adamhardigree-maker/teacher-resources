Navigating CTLS

Class Schedule

Every CVA course has a Class Schedule that students can access inside their CTLS classroom. It outlines the expected timeline for completing course content and is one of the most useful reference points a teacher has for monitoring pacing and making outreach specific.

Big Idea: Teachers don’t set or modify the Class Schedule — but referencing it before outreach makes your communication more targeted and more useful to students.
Overview

Your Pacing Reference Point

When you open the Progress Tracker and see a student who hasn’t submitted recently, the Class Schedule tells you how serious that gap actually is. Without it, you’re estimating. With it, you can see exactly where the student should be relative to a published course timeline — and that makes every conversation more grounded.

Students have access to the Class Schedule inside their CTLS classroom. That means when you reference it in outreach or announcements, you’re pointing them to something they can already see.

Three Ways to Use It

How Teachers Use the Class Schedule

1

Before Student Outreach

Check the Class Schedule before you send a message to a student who appears behind. It tells you which assignments or units the student should have completed by now, so your outreach can name specifics rather than speaking in generalities.

Specific outreach gets responses. “You’re missing Unit 3 work, which was due last week according to your Class Schedule” is more actionable than “please catch up.”

2

In Class Announcements

When you post a pacing reminder to all students, reference the Class Schedule by name. Directing students to something they can actually find is more useful than a general reminder to stay on track.

Students are more likely to take action when they have a concrete next step. “Check your Class Schedule” gives them one.

3

In Conversations with Local Schools

When a local school contact asks about a student’s progress, the Class Schedule gives you a shared reference point. You can speak to where the student should be in the course and where they actually are — a much more useful conversation than a raw grade.

A shared reference point makes collaboration more productive and the student’s situation easier to explain.

Why It Matters

Why This Matters

Anchors Outreach

Knowing where a student should be makes your intervention timely and specific rather than reactive and general.

Student-Facing

Students already have access to it, so referencing it in communication reinforces a tool they should be using on their own.

Supports Collaboration

It gives teachers and local school staff a common language when discussing student progress and next steps.

Quick Takeaways

What to Remember

  • ✓The Class Schedule is published inside every student’s CTLS classroom — students can access it directly.
  • ✓Teachers don’t set or modify it — but it should inform how you read progress data and conduct outreach.
  • ✓Reference it by name in announcements and outreach so students know where to look.
  • ✓Use it before contacting local schools so you can speak specifically to where a student is relative to course expectations.
