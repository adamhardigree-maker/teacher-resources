Teacher Communication

Communicating Deadlines to Students

Deadlines exist inside the Class Schedule — but students don’t always check it, and they won’t always know when they’re falling behind. That communication is the teacher’s job. It doesn’t require a lot of time. It requires consistency.

Big Idea: Use the right channel for the right situation — announcements for broad reminders, direct outreach for individual concerns, and the feedback box for assignment-specific deadlines.
Overview

Three Channels, Three Purposes

CVA teachers have three main channels for communicating deadline expectations to students. Each serves a different purpose and reaches students in a different context. Using all three — at the right moments — is what makes deadline communication feel consistent rather than sporadic.

Communication Channels

When and How to Communicate

1

Class Announcements

Use announcements for broad, proactive reminders to all students. Post at natural pacing checkpoints — the end of a unit, the midpoint of the term, or before a common submission window. Reference the Class Schedule by name so students know where to find the details.

Announcements reach everyone at once. They work best as early signals — before students are behind, not after.

Sample Announcement
We’re approaching the end of Unit 2. Check your Class Schedule to make sure your work is on track. If you have missing assignments, now is a great time to reach out — I’m happy to help you get caught up.
2

Direct Student Outreach

Use direct outreach for individual students who are visibly behind based on the Progress Tracker. Be specific: name the missing work, reference the Class Schedule, and include a deadline. Keep the tone supportive — the goal is to remove barriers, not add pressure.

Specific outreach gets responses. A student who receives a message naming their missing work is far more likely to act than one who receives a general nudge.

Sample Outreach Message
Hi [Name], I noticed you haven’t submitted [assignment] yet and wanted to check in. According to the Class Schedule, this work was due [date]. Please reach out if you have any questions — I’m happy to help you get caught up.
3

The Feedback Box

Use the feedback box for deadline expectations tied to a specific assignment — especially resubmissions. This is the one place a student is guaranteed to see a message about their own work. Always name a specific date, not an open-ended window.

Open-ended urgency doesn’t move students. A specific date does.

Sample Feedback Box Message
Your resubmission is due by [date]. Please review the feedback above, revise your work, and resubmit before that date. Reach out if you have any questions.
Principles

Four Principles for Effective Deadline Communication

Be Specific

“You have three missing assignments” is more useful than “please catch up.” Name what’s missing when you can.

Reference the Schedule

Point students to the Class Schedule so they have something concrete to act on beyond your message.

Name a Deadline

Always give a specific date when you can. Open-ended urgency rarely produces action.

Stay Supportive

Frame outreach as help, not a warning. Students respond better when the message signals you’re on their side.

Use the Right Channel

Match your message to the situation: broad reminder → announcement; individual concern → outreach; assignment-specific → feedback box.

Be Consistent

One strong message at the start of the term is less effective than steady, well-timed communication throughout.

Quick Reference

Choosing the Right Channel

Situation Best Channel Key Elements to Include
Pacing checkpoint for all students Class Announcement Reference Class Schedule, general deadline reminder, invitation to reach out
Individual student visibly behind Direct Outreach Student’s name, specific missing work, Class Schedule reference, specific date, supportive tone
Resubmission opened for a student Feedback Box Specific resubmission deadline, summary of feedback, invitation to ask questions
Student has not responded to prior outreach Direct Outreach (follow-up) Reference prior message, updated deadline, escalation note if needed
Quick Takeaways

What to Remember

  • ✓Announcements are for broad, proactive reminders — post them at pacing checkpoints before students fall behind.
  • ✓Direct outreach should name specifics — the student’s missing work, the Class Schedule, and a concrete date.
  • ✓The feedback box is the most reliable channel for assignment-specific deadlines — students see it when they check their grade.
  • ✓Always reference the Class Schedule so students have something they can find and act on themselves.
  • ✓Consistent, specific, and supportive communication is more effective than occasional general reminders.
