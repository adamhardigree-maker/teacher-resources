Teacher Communication

Communicating Deadlines to Students

Deadlines exist inside the Class Schedule, but students do not always check it or recognize when they are falling behind. Consistent teacher communication helps students know what to check, what to complete, and when to ask for help.

Overview

Use the Right Channel for the Right Situation

CVA teachers have three main channels for communicating deadline expectations to students. Each serves a different purpose and reaches students in a different context. Using all three at the right moments helps deadline communication feel consistent rather than sporadic.

Big Idea: Use announcements for broad reminders, direct outreach for individual concerns, and the feedback box for assignment-specific deadlines.

Communication Channels

When and How to Communicate

Use these three channels together to create a clear, supportive communication routine for students.

Channel 1

Class Announcements

Use Class Announcements for broad, proactive reminders to all students. Post at natural pacing checkpoints, such as the end of a unit, the midpoint of the term, or before a common submission window. Reference the Class Schedule by name so students know where to find the details.

Best use: Early reminders before students are behind.

Sample Announcement

We are approaching the end of Unit 2. Check your Class Schedule to make sure your work is on track. If you have missing assignments, now is a great time to reach out. I am happy to help you get caught up.

Channel 2

Direct Student Outreach

Use direct outreach for individual students who are visibly behind based on the Progress Tracker. Be specific: name the missing work, reference the Class Schedule, and include a deadline. Keep the tone supportive so the message removes barriers rather than adding pressure.

Best use: Individual concerns, missing work, or lack of progress.

Sample Outreach Message

Hi [Name], I noticed you have not submitted [assignment] yet and wanted to check in. According to the Class Schedule, this work was due [date]. Please reach out if you have any questions. I am happy to help you get caught up.

Channel 3

Feedback Box

Use the feedback box for deadline expectations tied to a specific assignment, especially resubmissions. This is one of the most reliable places for a student to see a message about their own work. Always name a specific date instead of giving an open-ended window.

Best use: Assignment-specific reminders or resubmission deadlines.

Sample Feedback Box Message

Your resubmission is due by [date]. Please review the feedback above, revise your work, and resubmit before that date. Reach out if you have any questions.

Principles

Four Principles for Effective Deadline Communication

Be Specific

“You have three missing assignments” is more useful than “please catch up.” Name what is missing when you can.

Reference the Schedule

Point students to the Class Schedule so they have something concrete to find and act on beyond your message.

Name a Deadline

Always give a specific date when you can. Open-ended urgency rarely produces action.

Stay Supportive

Frame outreach as help, not a warning. Students respond better when the message signals you are on their side.

Use the Right Channel

Match your message to the situation: broad reminder, individual concern, or assignment-specific deadline.

Be Consistent

One strong message at the start of the term is less effective than steady, well-timed communication throughout.

Quick Takeaways

What to Remember

✓

Announcements are for broad, proactive reminders at pacing checkpoints before students fall behind.

✓

Direct outreach should name specifics, including the student’s missing work, the Class Schedule, and a concrete date.

✓

The feedback box is best for assignment-specific deadlines because students see it when they check their grade or resubmission.

✓

Always reference the Class Schedule so students have something they can find and act on themselves.

✓

Consistent, specific, and supportive communication is more effective than occasional general reminders.
